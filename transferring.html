The Basics

What Is the Transfer Portal?

The NCAA Transfer Portal is a database where student-athletes can register their intent to transfer to another school. Once you enter, any NCAA member school can contact you directly — and your current school must release your contact information within two business days.

Entering the portal does not mean you are committed to leaving. You can withdraw and remain at your current institution. However, timing and communication matter enormously — every decision you make in the portal has academic and athletic consequences.

Who Can Enter

Any NCAA student-athlete, regardless of sport or year, can enter the portal. Walk-ons, scholarship athletes, and partial scholarship athletes are all eligible.

One-Time Transfer Exception

Most athletes now have a one-time transfer exception allowing immediate eligibility at the new school without sitting out a year — a major rule change from the old model.

Portal Windows

Specific sports have defined transfer windows. Tennis has its own window. Entering outside the window may affect your eligibility timeline, so know your sport's dates.

Know the Rules

Eligibility & Timing

Eligibility rules for transfers have changed significantly in recent years. Understanding your specific situation — scholarship status, seasons of competition used, academic standing, and the one-time exception — is essential before making any moves.

  • 01Check your seasons of competition. You are allowed four seasons of competition. Know how many you have used and how many remain — this shapes which schools will realistically pursue you.
  • 02Confirm academic eligibility. You must be in good academic standing to be eligible to compete at a new school. Contact your academic advisor before entering the portal.
  • 03Understand the one-time exception. If this is your first transfer, you are likely eligible to compete immediately at your new school in most sports — but verify this applies to your specific situation.
  • 04Check your sport's transfer window. Different sports have different portal windows. Missing your sport's window can delay your eligibility by a full academic year.
  • 05Review your current scholarship terms. Some scholarships have language about transfers. Talk to your compliance office — not just your coaches — to understand your financial situation.
Finding Your Fit

Evaluating Programs

The transfer process is your second chance to find the right environment — academically, athletically, and personally. Be more strategic this time. You know what you want, what you need, and what didn't work at your last school.

Athletic Fit

Where do you fit in the lineup? Are you transferring to compete for a spot or to walk into one? Be honest with yourself and with coaches about where you realistically land on a roster.

Academic Fit

Can you get into your intended major? Will your credits transfer? Meet with the registrar at prospective schools early — credit loss can delay graduation significantly.

Culture & Coaching Style

The coaching relationship is often why athletes transfer. Ask hard questions about daily culture, practice structure, and how coaches handle adversity before committing.

Financial Aid

Scholarship offers at the new school start fresh. Don't assume your existing package transfers. Get financial aid details in writing before making any decision.

Coach Outreach

Communicating with Coaches

Once you are in the portal, coaches can reach out to you — but you should also be proactive. A well-crafted outreach message, a clean highlight reel, and a strong academic profile open more doors than waiting for the phone to ring.

  • 01Update your athletic profile. Make sure your highlight video is current, well-edited, and clearly shows your best competitive moments. Coaches make quick decisions from video.
  • 02Send targeted outreach. Identify 15–20 schools that are a realistic fit. Send personalized emails — not mass blasts — that explain why their program specifically interests you.
  • 03Be transparent about your situation. Coaches will find out why you transferred. Get ahead of it with an honest, professional explanation that frames your decision positively.
  • 04Ask the right questions on visits. Ask about practice structure, travel schedule, academic support, and team culture — not just scholarship amounts. The answers reveal a lot about the program.
  • 05Get everything in writing. Verbal scholarship offers are not binding. Before withdrawing from the portal or signing anything, confirm scholarship details and roster spot expectations in writing.
Making the Move

Making the Decision

Choosing where to transfer is one of the most consequential decisions of your college athletic career. Pressure from coaches, timelines, and limited scholarship availability can make it feel rushed — but getting it right matters more than getting it done fast.

Don't Rush

The portal moves fast, but you don't have to take the first offer. If a school is right for you, they will wait. If they won't, that tells you something about the environment you'd be entering.

Talk to Current Players

Reach out directly to current team members — not just the players coaches introduce you to. Ask about real daily life, training load, academic support, and coaching relationships.

Include Your Support System

Parents, coaches, and mentors who know you well can offer perspective you can't see from inside the process. Make this decision with input, not in isolation.

Trust Your Gut

After the research, the visits, and the conversations — trust your instincts. You know what environment brings out your best. Choose the place that feels like a genuine opportunity, not just an escape.
